The Strait, the Yen, and the Unwind Nobody Sees Coming

Everyone is watching oil in dollars. The real tail risk is oil in yen.
On March 19, the Bank of Japan held its policy rate at 0.75% in an 8-to-1 vote, with board member Hajime Takata dissenting in favor of an immediate hike to 1.0%.[1] The same day, Brent crude spiked above $110 a barrel after Israeli and American strikes hit Iran’s South Pars gas field — the largest natural gas reserve on earth.[2] These two events, occurring within hours of each other, are not separate stories. They are the same story. And they are converging toward a scenario that global markets are dangerously underpricing: a yen-denominated oil shock that triggers the violent reverse carry trade unwind I have been warning about for months.
The Strait of Hormuz: Japan’s Existential Chokepoint
Japan imports approximately 95% of its crude oil from the Middle East.[3] Around 70% transits the Strait of Hormuz — a two-mile-wide chokepoint Iran has effectively disrupted since hostilities began on February 28.[4] Of the major Asian importers, Japan is the single most vulnerable, with 87% of its total energy from imported fossil fuels — the highest dependency among all major economies.[5]


Japan has already begun releasing emergency oil reserves, drawing on private-sector stockpiles equivalent to 15 days of consumption.[6] Total reserves stand at 254 days, but the physical crude market in Asia has completely disconnected from paper futures. Dubai crude hit an all-time high above $150 a barrel last week. Oman crude settled above $152. Meanwhile, WTI sits around $96 — an unprecedented $50-plus spread that normally runs $5 to $8.[2]


That divergence tells you everything. The barrels Japan needs are not the barrels priced in New York. Japan is paying the war premium in full.
Oil in Yen: The Mechanism Nobody Is Pricing
Here is the dynamic the financial media is ignoring: Japan does not buy oil in yen. It buys oil in dollars. When oil spikes and the yen simultaneously weakens, the cost of energy does not rise linearly — it compounds. Energy shock and balance-of-payments shock at the same time.
The USD/JPY pair surged to 159.74 on March 13, the upper bound of a volatile 154-to-160 corridor that has defined the first quarter of 2026.[7] At 159 yen to the dollar, a $110 barrel of Brent costs Japan roughly Ą17,500 — compared to Ą11,000 at the start of the year when oil was $70 and the yen traded near 157. That is a 60% increase in the yen cost of crude in under three months. The combination is a double hit on Japan’s current account — the precise mechanism that forces Japanese institutional investors to repatriate capital from overseas positions.


This is the reverse carry trade. For years, Japanese investors borrowed cheaply in yen and deployed capital into higher-yielding U.S. and global assets. BCA Research has called the yen carry trade a “ticking time bomb,” noting its vulnerability to a massive unwind.[8] Apollo Academy data confirms yen lending to offshore financial centers remains elevated — the structural footprint is enormous.[9] A yen-priced oil spike blows out the import bill, pressures the currency, and forces repatriation. The trigger is not the BOJ raising rates. It is the yen-denominated cost of energy making the carry trade’s funding leg untenable.
Takaichi’s Political Gamble: Stimulus Into an Inflationary Shock
What makes the current setup particularly dangerous is the political backdrop. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, who assumed office in late 2025, is a devoted disciple of Abenomics — the reflationary playbook that combined massive fiscal stimulus with ultra-loose monetary policy.[10] She has pushed through a record Ą122.3 trillion budget for fiscal 2026, the first to exceed Ą120 trillion.[11] Japan’s total government debt hit Ą1,342 trillion ($8.6 trillion) at the end of 2025 — more than twice GDP.[12]


Takaichi has stacked the BOJ board with doves: Asada joins in late March replacing Noguchi, while Sato replaces the hawkish Nakagawa in June.[13] Former BOJ Governor Kuroda — the architect of Abenomics-era monetary policy — has warned her spending plans “could stoke an inflationary upswing” and that Japan should be raising rates roughly twice a year.[14]
When the man who invented the bazooka tells you the new prime minister is being reckless, you should listen.
Takaichi won her snap election on February 8 with the LDP capturing 316 seats — the strongest single-party showing since WWII.[15] She campaigned on suspending the 8% consumption tax on food, at roughly Ą5 trillion (~$32 billion) in annual revenue loss.[16] The timing is catastrophic. She is running demand-side stimulus into a supply-side energy shock. Headline CPI fell to 1.5% in January, but core-core inflation only eased to 2.6%.[15] Rice prices are still up 27.9% year-on-year. Strip away the subsidies and the true picture looks very different.
The BOJ’s Impossible Position
Governor Ueda’s dilemma is acute. The BOJ’s March 19 statement flagged “potential inflationary pressures from higher oil prices” as a reason for caution.[1] But if oil stays above $100 and the yen remains near 159, imported inflation will re-accelerate regardless of domestic demand. Ueda told parliament on March 17 that underlying inflation is “gradually accelerating toward the 2% target.”[17] That forecast assumed a very different oil environment.


The BOJ cannot raise rates into an energy shock without crushing consumption. It cannot hold rates while the yen weakens without letting imported inflation run hot. And it cannot unwind bond purchases while the government issues record debt at assumed interest rates of 3.0% — the highest in 29 years.[11][18] Takata’s lone dissent on March 19 may be the last hawkish stand the board sees for some time.
Every path leads to pain. The question is which kind.
The Lead-Lag Dynamic: Phase 2 Has Begun
Watch oil priced in yen, not oil priced in dollars. That is the variable that determines whether this remains a contained geopolitical premium or mutates into a systemic event.
I have argued for months that the reverse carry trade would unfold in two phases. Phase 1 was preparation: Japanese institutions quietly selling U.S. Treasuries to build yen reserves and prepare to defend the currency. We saw this play out through late 2025, when Japanese investors sold foreign assets in December and net foreign outflows from Treasuries hit $27 billion.[21] U.S. 10-year yields climbed roughly 20 basis points in the early weeks of the conflict — not because of inflation expectations, but because Japan and other creditors were raising cash.[22]
Phase 2 is different. Phase 2 is the return of Treasuries as a flight-to-safety trade — the paradox where the very bonds Japan was selling now become the destination as the reverse carry trade accelerates and global risk assets crack. We may already be in it. The 10-year yield fell to a four-month low of 3.97% in early March as investors rotated out of equities and into government bonds.[23] Japan’s own Treasury holdings surged $39.8 billion in January alone, climbing to $1.225 trillion — the largest monthly increase in over a year.[24]
This is the tell. When Japan is simultaneously the largest foreign holder of U.S. Treasuries and the most vulnerable economy to a yen-priced oil shock, its capital flows become the transmission mechanism for global contagion. Phase 1 pushed yields higher. Phase 2 pulls them lower — but violently, and alongside equity and credit market liquidation. The August 2024 unwind — when a single BOJ rate hike sent U.S. momentum stocks into freefall — was the dress rehearsal.[20]
If Brent stays above $100 (and analysts are now openly discussing $150 to $200 scenarios[19]) while the yen remains pinned near 159, the cost of crude to Japan is approaching levels that trigger forced repatriation. When that happens, it will not be orderly. The lead is oil in yen. The lag is everything else — U.S. equities, credit spreads, the dollar itself. And Treasuries become the last safe harbor in a storm that Japan’s own capital flows created.
Takaichi’s political class is running fiscal stimulus into an energy war. The BOJ is frozen. The carry trade is sitting on a powder keg with a lit fuse. And Phase 2 has started.
Few understand this.
